Outdoor Planter questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best outdoor planter for you depends on your climate, plant type, and aesthetic. Choose a material that can handle your local weather (fiberglass and resin are most durable in freezing conditions), ensure drainage holes are present or can be added, and size the pot to give roots room to grow without being too heavy to move.

What material should I choose for outdoor planters?

Terracotta is classic and porous, which helps prevent overwatering, but it cracks easily when moisture freezes and expands. Plastic and resin planters are very lightweight and budget-friendly, though cheaper plastics can become brittle and fade after a few seasons of strong sun. Fiberglass is widely praised for being both lightweight and extremely durable, it won’t crack in frost or fade quickly, and it costs more but often lasts for years. Wood planters have a natural look but require internal liners to prevent rot, and concrete is extremely heavy and sturdy but hard to move once placed.

How do I ensure proper drainage?

Every outdoor planter should have at least one drainage hole. If yours doesn’t, you can drill one (use a masonry bit for ceramic or a standard drill bit for plastic). Without a hole, water pools at the bottom and rots roots. Elevate the pot on feet or small stones so water can escape freely; avoid letting the pot sit in a full saucer. For plants that like consistent moisture, a self-watering planter with a reservoir is a smart alternative, just be sure the plant species tolerates damp soil.

What size planter should I get?

A good rule of thumb is to choose a pot that is 2 to 4 inches wider in diameter than the plant’s current root ball. For larger shrubs or perennials, go for an 18-inch or bigger container to allow several years of growth. Bigger planters hold more soil, which means they retain moisture longer, that’s helpful for thirsty plants and hot climates, but it also means heavier pots. Consider where the planter will live: a very large pot might be tough to move or fit on a balcony.

How do I protect outdoor planters from frost?

Porous materials like terracotta and unglazed ceramic are most vulnerable to freeze-thaw cycles. Before the first hard frost, move these planters into a sheltered spot, or wrap them in bubble wrap or frost cloth. Fiberglass and resin are naturally frost-proof and need no extra protection. For large concrete or wood planters that can’t be moved, insulate the inside with a layer of packing peanuts or bubble wrap before adding soil, or bring tender plants inside and cover the pot with a tarp on freezing nights.

Should I get self-watering planters?

Self-watering planters are a great convenience for people who travel often or live in very hot, dry areas. They have a built-in reservoir that wicks water up to the roots, so you can go days or even a week longer between waterings. The trade-off is that they can encourage overwatering for plants that prefer dry conditions, succulents and cacti, for example, do poorly in them. They also tend to be made of plastic or resin, so check that the material is UV-resistant if the planter will get full sun.

How do I choose a planter that won't blow over in wind?

Weight is the key factor. Lightweight plastic and fiberglass pots need to be made stable by filling them with heavy soil or adding a layer of gravel at the bottom. Some resin planters come with wide, low-profile bases that minimize tipping risk. Tall, narrow planters are inherently less stable. For exposed balconies or windy patios, look for a planter with a broad footprint or go with concrete or thick-walled ceramic, they’re heavy enough to stay put even when fully planted.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need to line my wooden planter?

Yes, lining a wooden planter with plastic sheeting or a pond liner prevents soil from staying in direct contact with the wood, which slows rot and significantly extends the life of the planter. Just remember to punch a few drainage holes in the liner.

Can I leave my planter outside in winter?

That depends entirely on the material. Fiberglass and resin planters usually survive winter fine; terracotta and most glazed ceramic should be emptied and stored indoors or wrapped in frost cloth. Concrete can handle cold but may crack if water seeps into small fissures.

How often should I water plants in outdoor planters?

There is no single schedule, it depends on plant type, pot size, weather, and soil. A reliable check is to stick your finger an inch into the soil; if it feels dry, water thoroughly until it runs out the drainage holes. Self-watering planters can go much longer between refills.

What is the best soil for outdoor planters?

Always use a quality potting mix designed for containers, never garden soil. A good mix contains peat or coconut coir for moisture retention, perlite or vermiculite for drainage, and often a slow-release fertilizer. Some plants like succulents need a grittier, faster-draining blend.

Should I drill drainage holes if my planter doesn't have any?

Yes, unless you plan to use the container as a cachepot, meaning you’ll keep the actual nursery pot inside. Drilling holes is simple: use a carbide masonry bit for ceramic or terracotta, and a regular drill bit for plastic. Always wear safety goggles.

How do I prevent soil from escaping out drainage holes?

Place a coffee filter, a piece of window screen, or a layer of landscape fabric over the holes before adding soil. This keeps the dirt in while allowing water to drain freely. Avoid using gravel or pot shards, they can actually trap water by clogging the hole.

In shortThe right outdoor planter makes gardening easier and more enjoyable. Focus first on matching the material to your climate, then on size and drainage. With a little thought upfront, your plants will thrive and your patio will look great for seasons to come.
